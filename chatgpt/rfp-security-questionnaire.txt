# RFP / Security Questionnaire

Security-questionnaire operations, sanitized from a production sales-
engineering practice built around one inversion: the questionnaire is a
retrieval problem, not a writing problem.

## Rule 1: KB-first answering

Maintain a master FAQ - the production system's holds ~26 canonical
questions - covering the questions that actually recur: hosting and data
residency, encryption at rest/in transit, access control and SSO, audit
certifications (SOC 2 etc.), subprocessors, retention and deletion,
incident response, BCP/DR, pen-test cadence, vulnerability management.
Every new questionnaire is answered BY RETRIEVAL first: match each incoming
question to a canonical answer, adapt phrasing, done. In practice the
master FAQ covers 80-90% of any questionnaire; the remaining 10-20% get
written fresh AND evaluated for promotion into the FAQ (if a question
recurs twice, it's canonical now). The FAQ has one owner; answers carry a
last-verified date, re-verified quarterly - a stale answer in a security
review is worse than no answer, because it's discoverable as wrong.

## Rule 2: the decline threshold

Not every questionnaire deserves the hours. The production rule: below
$50K ARR potential, decline the CUSTOM questionnaire and send the standard
security packet (public certs + master FAQ export + trust-center link)
instead. Most sub-threshold requesters accept the packet happily - the
questionnaire was their process default, not their requirement. The
threshold converts "we spent 6 hours on a $15K deal's spreadsheet" from
a recurring accident into a policy decision someone actually made.

## Rule 3: NDA-gated evidence routing

Every piece of security evidence is classified into a routing tier, decided
once, not per-deal under pressure:

| Tier | Examples | Gate |
|---|---|---|
| Public | cert badges, high-level architecture, subprocessor list | none - link freely |
| NDA | SOC 2 report, pen-test executive summary, DR test results | signed NDA on file, verified before send |
| Never-send | full pen-test findings, internal runbooks, incident postmortems | summarize under NDA; originals do not leave |

The tier table means the account team never improvises disclosure at
11 PM before a deadline - which is exactly when the full pen-test report
gets attached to an email "just to be helpful."

## If/then

- If a question has no KB match and smells one-off -> answer fresh, tag it;
  second occurrence promotes it to the master FAQ.
- If a question asks for never-send material -> respond with the NDA-tier
  summary and a call offer; a walkthrough closes the concern faster than
  a document that then lives in their vendor folder forever.
- If the deadline is under 5 business days and the questionnaire is 100+
  questions -> negotiate scope first ("here's our packet covering 80% -
  which remaining sections are decision-critical?"). Full-speed on an
  unscoped questionnaire is how errors ship.
- If an answer would be aspirational ("we're rolling that out") -> write
  the truthful current state + dated roadmap note. Aspirational answers in
  security reviews become contract exhibits.

## Escalation

- Any question about a past incident, ongoing vulnerability, or legal
  matter -> security/legal owner reviews the answer before send, no
  exceptions, regardless of deadline.
- Same "missing" control asked by 3+ prospects in a quarter -> that's a
  roadmap signal with revenue attached; brief the security owner with
  the deal list.

## Worked example

Tuesday: 140-question questionnaire lands, enterprise prospect, ~$150K ARR
potential (clears the $50K bar - proceed), deadline 10 business days.
Retrieval pass, 2.5 hours: 118 questions matched to the master FAQ (84%),
phrasing adapted. 22 remain: 14 written fresh (2 look recurring - tagged
for promotion); 5 request the SOC 2 report -> NDA check: on file, verified,
sent with expiring link; 3 ask for detailed pen-test findings -> never-send
tier: NDA summary attached + walkthrough call offered (they take the call;
concern closed in 25 minutes). One question about a 2024 incident ->
security owner reviews the answer before send. Delivered day 7 of 10.
Same week, a $20K-potential prospect sends a 90-question sheet -> declined
per threshold, standard packet sent - accepted without complaint, 6 hours
saved. The FAQ gains 2 questions; quarterly re-verification is 3 weeks out.

## Definition of done

- [ ] Retrieval pass done before any fresh writing; coverage % noted
- [ ] Fresh answers tagged; recurring ones promoted to the master FAQ
- [ ] Every evidence send matched its tier; NDA verified before NDA-tier sends
- [ ] Nothing aspirational shipped as current state
- [ ] Incident/vulnerability/legal answers reviewed by the owner
- [ ] Sub-threshold requests got the packet, not the hours

## Anti-patterns

- **Writing from scratch under deadline.** Fresh-writing 140 answers at
  11 PM produces contradictions with the last questionnaire you sent -
  and security reviewers at big prospects DO diff vendors' answers over time.
- **The heroic yes.** Answering every custom questionnaire regardless of deal
  size feels customer-obsessed and is actually a subsidy from your biggest
  deals to your smallest. The threshold is the honest version.
- **Disclosure improvisation.** Deciding at send-time what the prospect can
  see guarantees the one 11 PM mistake that matters. The tier table is
  boring; boring is the point.
- **Aspirational answers.** "We're SOC 2 compliant" (report in progress)
  reads as a lie in month 11 of the contract when the auditor asks for the
  report dated before signature.

## Setup (first run, ~half a day - amortizes forever)

1. Pull your last 3-5 completed questionnaires; every question asked twice
   goes into the master FAQ draft. You'll land near 20-30 canonical answers.
2. Assign the FAQ owner and stamp every answer with last-verified + source
   (who confirmed it).
3. Build the evidence tier table with security/legal in the room - one
   meeting, decided once.
4. Assemble the standard packet (public tier + FAQ export + trust links)
   so the decline path is one email.
5. Set the ARR threshold with sales leadership so the decline is POLICY,
   not the SE's personal courage every time.

## Swappable parameters

- Decline threshold: `$50K ARR` -> your sales-engineering cost per questionnaire
- Master FAQ size: `~26 questions` -> grows by the promotion rule, not by decree
- Re-verification: quarterly -> your compliance cadence
- Rush bar: `<5 days for 100+ questions` -> your team's honest throughput
