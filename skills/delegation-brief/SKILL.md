# Delegation Brief

Most delegated tasks fail at the handoff, not the execution: the work comes
back and it's the wrong thing — because "done" was never defined and the
constraint that mattered was in your head. A brief takes 5 minutes; a
boomeranged task costs a full round trip.

**The rule: never delegate anything expected to take more than 30 minutes
without a written brief containing all four sections below.** Under 30
minutes, a one-line ask is fine — a brief that costs more than the task
is theater.

## The four sections

1. **Context** — why this task exists, in 2-4 sentences. Not the history,
   the *purpose*: the delegate makes a dozen micro-decisions you didn't
   anticipate, and purpose is what makes them come out right.

2. **Constraints** — what must NOT happen: budget ceilings, systems not to
   touch, people not to contact, deadlines, tone. Unstated constraints are
   the #1 boomerang cause — work that's "done" but violates something you
   assumed was obvious.

3. **Definition of done** — 2-5 *checkable* conditions, each verifiable by
   the delegate WITHOUT asking you. "Doc shared with edit access to the
   leadership group" is checkable; "doc looks good" is not. If you can't
   write a checkable DoD, you don't know what you want yet — figure that
   out before delegating.

4. **Escalation triggers** — the specific conditions where they stop and
   come back instead of improvising. Always include a spend/scope ceiling
   and a time trigger ("more than 2 hours in → stop and check in with what
   you've found"). Triggers are what make delegation safe — without them
   your options are micromanaging or hoping.

## Worked example

> **Task:** Get quotes for team offsite venues (12 people, 2 nights, March).
> **Context:** First offsite since the reorg; goal is planning time
> together, not a perk. One big work room matters more than fancy rooms.
> **Constraints:** Budget ceiling $14K all-in. Within 2h travel of the
> office. Ask as an individual, not as [Company]. Don't book anything.
> **Definition of done:** A doc with 3-5 venues, each with total cost
> (rooms + meeting space + meals), work-room capacity, availability for
> the two candidate weeks, cancellation terms. Shared with me and ops by
> Friday EOD.
> **Escalation triggers:** Any venue wants a deposit to hold a date → come
> back to me. Nothing viable under $14K → stop after 3 confirmed data
> points, don't stretch the budget. More than 3 hours in → check in.

Five checkable DoD conditions — the delegate can self-grade before
returning, which is exactly what prevents the boomerang.

## Validation (definition of done for the brief itself)

1. All four sections present — missing one makes it a hope, not a handoff.
2. Every DoD condition checkable by the delegate alone (no "looks good").
3. At least one escalation trigger has a number in it (hours, dollars,
   or count).
4. Context contains purpose only — if it's longer than the other three
   sections combined, it's a memo, not a brief.

## Adapting this

Works identically for human delegates and AI agents — agents need the
escalation triggers *more*, since they improvise confidently. A DoD the
agent can self-grade is what makes agent delegation safe. The
30-minute threshold and the numbers-in-triggers rule are the structure;
the values are swappable to your context.
